Since we already know the Taylor series for \(\cos(x) \) about \(x=\frac{\pi}{2} \) we can obtain the \(4^{\text{th}} \) degree Taylor polynomial for \(f(x)=x\cos(x) \) about \(x=\frac{\pi}{2}. \) as follows

\begin{align*} f(x) \amp = x\cos(x) \\ \amp = (x-\frac{\pi}{2})\cos(x)+\frac{\pi}{2}\cos(x) \\ \amp = (x-\frac{\pi}{2}) \big[-(x-\frac{\pi}{2}) + \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^3}{3!} - \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^5}{5!} + \dots \big] + \frac{\pi}{2}\big[ -(x-\frac{\pi}{2}) + \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^3}{3!} - \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^5}{5!} + \dots \big] \\ \amp = \big[-(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^2 + \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^4}{3!} - \dots \big] + \big[ -\frac{\pi}{2}(x-\frac{\pi}{2}) + \frac{\pi}{2}\frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^3}{3!} - \dots \big]. \end{align*}

Thus the \(4^{\text{th}} \) degree Taylor polynomial is

\begin{equation*} T_{4}(x) = -\frac{\pi}{2} (x-\frac{\pi}{2}) - (x-\frac{\pi}{2})^2 + \frac{\pi}{2} \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^3}{3!} + \frac{(x-\frac{\pi}{2})^4}{3!}. \end{equation*}
